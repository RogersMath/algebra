Linear Equations & Coordinate Geometry

Slope

m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}

  • Slope represents the rate of change between two points.
  • You may use any two points on the line — the result will always be the same.
  • Be consistent: if you do y₂ − y₁ on top, you must do x₂ − x₁ on the bottom.
  • Positive slopes rise left to right; negative slopes fall left to right.
  • A horizontal line has slope 0. A vertical line has undefined slope.

Find the slope through (1, 2) and (4, 7).

m = \frac{7 - 2}{4 - 1} = \frac{5}{3}

Find the slope through (−3, 4) and (2, −1).

m = \frac{-1 - 4}{2 - (-3)} = \frac{-5}{5} = -1

  • Interpreting steepness and direction of a line on a graph.
  • Understanding rate of change in real-world contexts: speed, cost per unit, temperature change, population growth.
  • Determining whether a relationship is increasing, decreasing, or constant.

Related: Point Slope, Slope Intercept, Distance, Midpoint, Average Rate Of Change

Point–Slope Form

y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)

  • Use point–slope form when you know a point on the line and its slope.
  • (x₁, y₁) is any known point on the line; m is the slope.
  • This form is especially useful when writing the equation of a line from two points.
  • You can convert to slope–intercept form by distributing and solving for y.
  • Either of the two given points can serve as (x₁, y₁) — both give the same line.

Write the equation of a line with slope 3 through (2, 5).

y - 5 = 3(x - 2)

Write the equation through (−1, 4) and (3, −2). First find slope, then apply form.

m = \frac{-2-4}{3-(-1)} = \frac{-6}{4} = -\frac{3}{2} \quad\Rightarrow\quad y - 4 = -\frac{3}{2}(x + 1)

  • Writing the equation of a tangent line in calculus preparation.
  • Modeling any linear situation when you have a known data point and a rate of change.
  • Converting between forms of linear equations.

Related: Slope, Slope Intercept

Slope–Intercept Form

y = mx + b

  • m is the slope — the rate at which y changes for every 1-unit increase in x.
  • b is the y-intercept — the value of y when x = 0 (where the line crosses the y-axis).
  • This is the most common form for graphing a line quickly.
  • To graph: plot (0, b), then use slope m = rise/run to find a second point.
  • If an equation is not in this form, solve for y to reveal m and b.

Identify slope and y-intercept of y = 2x − 3.

m = 2, \quad b = -3

Rewrite 3x + 2y = 8 in slope–intercept form.

2y = -3x + 8 \quad\Rightarrow\quad y = -\frac{3}{2}x + 4

  • Graphing lines by hand using slope and y-intercept.
  • Interpreting linear models: the slope is the unit rate, and b is the starting value.
  • Writing equations from graphs by reading m and b directly.

Related: Slope, Point Slope, Standard Form

Midpoint Formula

M = \left(\frac{x_1 + x_2}{2},\, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2}\right)

  • The midpoint is the exact center point between two given points.
  • Average the x-coordinates for the new x; average the y-coordinates for the new y.
  • The result is always a coordinate pair (x, y), not a single number.
  • Order of the points doesn't matter — averaging is commutative.
  • Useful when you need to bisect a segment or find the center of a diameter.

Find the midpoint between (2, 4) and (8, 10).

M = \left(\frac{2+8}{2}, \frac{4+10}{2}\right) = (5, 7)

Find the midpoint between (−3, 1) and (5, −7).

M = \left(\frac{-3+5}{2}, \frac{1+(-7)}{2}\right) = (1, -3)

  • Finding the center of a circle given the endpoints of a diameter.
  • Bisecting a line segment in geometry and construction problems.
  • Locating halfway points in navigation, mapping, or data interpolation.

Related: Slope, Distance

Distance Formula

d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}

  • The distance formula is derived directly from the Pythagorean theorem.
  • It gives the straight-line (Euclidean) distance between any two points.
  • Order of subtraction doesn't matter — the result is squared, so negatives cancel.
  • The answer is always non-negative.
  • If the two points share an x-value (vertical) or y-value (horizontal), the formula still works but simplifies.

Find the distance between (1, 2) and (4, 6).

d = \sqrt{(4-1)^2 + (6-2)^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5

Find the distance between (−2, 3) and (4, −5).

d = \sqrt{(4-(-2))^2 + (-5-3)^2} = \sqrt{36 + 64} = \sqrt{100} = 10

  • Verifying the classification of triangles (equilateral, isosceles, scalene).
  • Finding the radius of a circle given its center and a point on it.
  • GPS and mapping: calculating straight-line distance between coordinates.

Related: Slope, Midpoint

Quadratic Functions

Standard Form (Quadratic)

y = ax^2 + bx + c

  • Standard form is the most common way a quadratic function is written.
  • a determines the direction and width of the parabola: if a > 0 it opens up; if a < 0 it opens down.
  • The larger |a| is, the narrower the parabola.
  • c is the y-intercept — the value of the function when x = 0.
  • From standard form you can find the axis of symmetry using x = −b/(2a), and then the vertex.

Identify a, b, and c in y = 3x² − 5x + 2.

a = 3, \quad b = -5, \quad c = 2

Find the y-intercept of y = −2x² + 4x − 1.

\text{When } x = 0: \quad y = -2(0)^2 + 4(0) - 1 = -1

  • Modeling projectile motion: height as a function of time.
  • Describing profit/loss curves in business contexts.
  • Serving as the entry point for applying the quadratic formula.

Related: Vertex Form, Factored Form, Quadratic Formula, Axis Of Symmetry, Discriminant

Vertex Form

y = a(x - h)^2 + k

  • Vertex form reveals the vertex of the parabola immediately: the vertex is (h, k).
  • Watch the sign: y = a(x − 3)² + 5 has vertex (3, 5), not (−3, 5).
  • a still controls direction and width, just as in standard form.
  • If k > 0, the vertex is above the x-axis. If k < 0, it is below.
  • Convert from standard form to vertex form by completing the square.

Identify the vertex of y = 2(x − 4)² + 1.

\text{Vertex} = (4, 1), \quad \text{opens upward since } a = 2 > 0

Identify the vertex of y = −(x + 2)² − 3.

\text{Vertex} = (-2, -3), \quad \text{opens downward since } a = -1 < 0

  • Instantly reading the maximum or minimum value of a quadratic (the k-value).
  • Graphing parabolas quickly from a known vertex and direction.
  • Optimization problems: finding the peak height of a projectile, maximum profit, minimum cost.

Related: Standard Form, Factored Form, Axis Of Symmetry

Factored Form

y = a(x - r_1)(x - r_2)

  • Factored form reveals the zeros (x-intercepts) of the parabola directly: they are r₁ and r₂.
  • Set each factor equal to zero to find the roots: x − r₁ = 0 gives x = r₁.
  • The axis of symmetry is the average of the two roots: x = (r₁ + r₂) / 2.
  • If a quadratic cannot be factored over integers, use the quadratic formula instead.
  • a is the same leading coefficient as in standard and vertex form.

Identify the zeros of y = 2(x − 3)(x + 1).

x - 3 = 0 \Rightarrow x = 3 \qquad x + 1 = 0 \Rightarrow x = -1

Factor y = x² − 5x + 6 into factored form.

y = (x - 2)(x - 3) \quad \Rightarrow \quad r_1 = 2, \; r_2 = 3

  • Finding where a projectile lands (when height = 0).
  • Determining break-even points in revenue/cost models.
  • Solving quadratic equations by setting y = 0 and reading off roots.

Related: Standard Form, Vertex Form, Quadratic Formula, Difference Of Squares

Quadratic Formula

x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}

  • The quadratic formula solves any quadratic equation in standard form ax² + bx + c = 0.
  • The ± means there are potentially two solutions — one with + and one with −.
  • Always identify a, b, and c carefully before substituting. Watch signs.
  • The expression under the radical, b² − 4ac, is the discriminant. It tells you how many real solutions exist.
  • If the discriminant is negative, there are no real solutions (the parabola doesn't cross the x-axis).

Solve x² − 5x + 6 = 0 using the quadratic formula.

x = \frac{5 \pm \sqrt{25 - 24}}{2} = \frac{5 \pm 1}{2} \quad\Rightarrow\quad x = 3 \text{ or } x = 2

Solve 2x² + 3x − 2 = 0.

x = \frac{-3 \pm \sqrt{9 + 16}}{4} = \frac{-3 \pm 5}{4} \quad\Rightarrow\quad x = \tfrac{1}{2} \text{ or } x = -2

  • Solving any quadratic equation when factoring is not obvious or possible.
  • Finding the x-intercepts of any parabola.
  • Calculating time values in physics problems involving height and gravity.

Related: Standard Form, Discriminant, Factored Form

Discriminant

D = b^2 - 4ac

  • The discriminant is the expression inside the square root of the quadratic formula.
  • If D > 0: two distinct real solutions (the parabola crosses the x-axis twice).
  • If D = 0: exactly one real solution, a repeated root (the parabola touches the x-axis once).
  • If D < 0: no real solutions (the parabola does not cross the x-axis).
  • You can classify the number of solutions before solving — saving time on exams.

How many real solutions does 2x² − 4x + 2 = 0 have?

D = (-4)^2 - 4(2)(2) = 16 - 16 = 0 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \text{one repeated solution}

How many real solutions does x² + x + 1 = 0 have?

D = 1^2 - 4(1)(1) = 1 - 4 = -3 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \text{no real solutions}

  • Quickly determining whether a quadratic is solvable by real methods before attempting to solve.
  • Predicting whether a projectile reaches a given height.
  • Analyzing whether two curves intersect in algebra and calculus.

Related: Quadratic Formula, Standard Form

Axis of Symmetry

x = -\frac{b}{2a}

  • The axis of symmetry is the vertical line that divides a parabola into two mirror-image halves.
  • It always passes through the vertex — so x = −b/(2a) gives the x-coordinate of the vertex.
  • Substitute this x-value back into the original equation to find the y-coordinate of the vertex.
  • The axis of symmetry is also the average of the two x-intercepts (when they exist).
  • For a parabola in vertex form y = a(x − h)² + k, the axis of symmetry is simply x = h.

Find the axis of symmetry of y = 2x² − 8x + 3.

x = -\frac{-8}{2(2)} = \frac{8}{4} = 2

Then find the vertex by substituting x = 2 back in.

y = 2(2)^2 - 8(2) + 3 = 8 - 16 + 3 = -5 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \text{Vertex: } (2, -5)

  • Graphing a parabola efficiently — find axis, then vertex, then two symmetric points.
  • Solving optimization problems where you need the minimum or maximum output.
  • Verifying vertex form: h should match the axis of symmetry value.

Related: Standard Form, Vertex Form, Quadratic Formula

Exponents & Radicals

Product Rule (Exponents)

a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n}

  • When multiplying two powers with the same base, keep the base and add the exponents.
  • The bases must be identical — you cannot combine x³ · y² using this rule.
  • Works for integer, fractional, and negative exponents.
  • This rule extends to three or more factors: a² · a³ · a⁴ = a⁹.
  • Remember: multiply the bases? No — add the exponents. The base stays the same.

Simplify x³ · x⁵.

x^3 \cdot x^5 = x^{3+5} = x^8

Simplify 2³ · 2⁴.

2^3 \cdot 2^4 = 2^{3+4} = 2^7 = 128

  • Simplifying algebraic expressions in polynomials.
  • Combining exponential terms in scientific notation.
  • Working with monomials in polynomial multiplication.

Related: Quotient Rule, Power Rule, Fractional Exponent

Quotient Rule (Exponents)

\frac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n}

  • When dividing two powers with the same base, keep the base and subtract the exponents.
  • Always subtract the denominator's exponent from the numerator's: top minus bottom.
  • If m < n, the result is a negative exponent: a⁻² = 1/a².
  • If m = n, the result is a⁰ = 1 (for any nonzero base).
  • The bases must be the same — this rule doesn't apply to different bases.

Simplify x⁷ ÷ x³.

\frac{x^7}{x^3} = x^{7-3} = x^4

Simplify a² ÷ a⁵.

\frac{a^2}{a^5} = a^{2-5} = a^{-3} = \frac{1}{a^3}

  • Simplifying rational expressions with matching bases.
  • Reducing scientific notation expressions.
  • Deriving zero and negative exponent rules.

Related: Product Rule, Power Rule, Fractional Exponent

Power Rule (Exponents)

(a^m)^n = a^{mn}

  • When raising a power to another power, keep the base and multiply the exponents.
  • This is different from the product rule — here the exponents multiply, not add.
  • Extends to products inside parentheses: (ab)ⁿ = aⁿbⁿ.
  • Also extends to quotients: (a/b)ⁿ = aⁿ/bⁿ.
  • Common mistake: (x²)³ = x⁶, not x⁵. Multiply, don't add, when raising a power to a power.

Simplify (x³)⁴.

(x^3)^4 = x^{3 \cdot 4} = x^{12}

Simplify (2x²y³)³.

(2x^2y^3)^3 = 2^3 x^{2 \cdot 3} y^{3 \cdot 3} = 8x^6y^9

  • Simplifying expressions with nested exponents.
  • Expanding polynomial expressions involving powers of binomials (preparation for binomial theorem).
  • Working with scientific notation raised to a power.

Related: Product Rule, Quotient Rule, Fractional Exponent

Radical Product Rule

\sqrt{ab} = \sqrt{a} \cdot \sqrt{b}

  • The square root of a product equals the product of the individual square roots.
  • Use this to simplify radicals by factoring out perfect squares.
  • Also works in reverse — you can combine two radicals into one: √3 · √5 = √15.
  • Requires a ≥ 0 and b ≥ 0 when working in real numbers.
  • Common application: √50 = √(25 · 2) = √25 · √2 = 5√2.

Simplify √72.

\sqrt{72} = \sqrt{36 \cdot 2} = \sqrt{36} \cdot \sqrt{2} = 6\sqrt{2}

Multiply √3 · √12.

\sqrt{3} \cdot \sqrt{12} = \sqrt{36} = 6

  • Simplifying radical expressions to simplest form.
  • Rationalizing denominators in fractions.
  • Combining and comparing radical expressions.

Related: Fractional Exponent, Product Rule

Fractional Exponent

a^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{a}

  • A fractional exponent is just another way to write a root.
  • The denominator of the fraction tells you which root to take.
  • The general form is a^(m/n) = (ⁿ√a)^m = ⁿ√(aᵐ) — root first is usually easier.
  • Negative fractional exponents flip the base: a^(−1/n) = 1 / ⁿ√a.
  • Treat fractional exponents with the same rules as integer exponents (product, quotient, power).

Evaluate 8^(1/3).

8^{1/3} = \sqrt[3]{8} = 2

Evaluate 16^(3/4).

16^{3/4} = (\sqrt[4]{16})^3 = 2^3 = 8

  • Rewriting roots as exponents to apply exponent rules more easily.
  • Evaluating expressions involving roots without a calculator.
  • Connecting radical notation to exponential notation in higher mathematics.

Related: Radical Product, Product Rule, Power Rule

Polynomial Factoring

Difference of Squares

a^2 - b^2 = (a - b)(a + b)

  • This pattern only works for subtraction — a sum of squares (a² + b²) does not factor over the reals.
  • Both terms must be perfect squares: look for squared variables or perfect square coefficients.
  • The two factors are conjugates: one has a plus, the other has a minus.
  • When multiplied back out, the middle terms cancel, leaving only the difference of squares.
  • Check: is each term a perfect square? Is there a minus between them? If yes to both, apply this pattern.

Factor x² − 25.

x^2 - 25 = (x-5)(x+5)

Factor 4x² − 9.

4x^2 - 9 = (2x - 3)(2x + 3)

  • Quickly factoring expressions without using trial and error.
  • Solving equations like x² = 49 by recognizing x² − 49 = 0.
  • Simplifying rational expressions by canceling common factors after factoring.

Related: Factored Form, Sum Of Cubes, Difference Of Cubes

Sum of Cubes

a^3 + b^3 = (a + b)(a^2 - ab + b^2)

  • Memorize the pattern: the first factor matches the sign of the original (plus), and the trinomial follows SOAP — Same, Opposite, Always Positive.
  • SOAP: Same sign as original (+), Opposite sign (−ab), Always Positive (+b²).
  • The trinomial factor (a² − ab + b²) does not factor further over the reals.
  • Both terms must be perfect cubes: 1, 8, 27, 64, 125, ... and their variable equivalents.
  • Do not confuse with the difference of cubes — the only change is the sign of the linear factor.

Factor x³ + 8.

x^3 + 8 = x^3 + 2^3 = (x + 2)(x^2 - 2x + 4)

Factor 27x³ + 64.

27x^3 + 64 = (3x)^3 + 4^3 = (3x + 4)(9x^2 - 12x + 16)

  • Factoring cubic polynomials that match the sum-of-cubes pattern.
  • Simplifying rational expressions involving cubic terms.
  • Foundation for polynomial long division and higher-degree factoring.

Related: Difference Of Cubes, Difference Of Squares

Difference of Cubes

a^3 - b^3 = (a - b)(a^2 + ab + b^2)

  • Like the sum of cubes, use the SOAP mnemonic: Same, Opposite, Always Positive.
  • SOAP: Same sign as original (−), Opposite sign (+ab), Always Positive (+b²).
  • The trinomial factor (a² + ab + b²) does not factor further over the reals.
  • Identify a and b by taking the cube root of each term.
  • The only difference from sum of cubes is the sign of the linear factor and the middle term of the trinomial.

Factor x³ − 27.

x^3 - 27 = x^3 - 3^3 = (x - 3)(x^2 + 3x + 9)

Factor 8x³ − 125.

8x^3 - 125 = (2x)^3 - 5^3 = (2x - 5)(4x^2 + 10x + 25)

  • Factoring cubic expressions that fit the difference-of-cubes pattern.
  • Simplifying rational expressions with cubic denominators.
  • Solving cubic equations of the form a³ = b³.

Related: Sum Of Cubes, Difference Of Squares

Functions & Rates

Average Rate of Change

\frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}

  • The average rate of change measures how much a function's output changes per unit of input over an interval.
  • It is the slope of the secant line connecting the two points (a, f(a)) and (b, f(b)) on the graph.
  • For a linear function, the average rate of change is always equal to the slope m.
  • For a nonlinear function (like a parabola), the rate of change varies — this formula gives the average over [a, b].
  • This formula is the foundation of differential calculus — the derivative is the limit of the average rate of change as the interval shrinks to zero.

Find the average rate of change of f(x) = x² on [1, 4].

\frac{f(4) - f(1)}{4 - 1} = \frac{16 - 1}{3} = \frac{15}{3} = 5

Find the average rate of change of f(x) = 2x + 3 on [0, 5].

\frac{f(5) - f(0)}{5 - 0} = \frac{13 - 3}{5} = \frac{10}{5} = 2

  • Calculating average speed, average velocity, or average acceleration over a time interval.
  • Comparing growth rates of different functions over the same interval.
  • Approximating instantaneous rates of change — the gateway concept to calculus.
